Autonomous Inertial Navigation System

The Iowa State University high powered rocketry team, CyLaunch, participates in the annual NASA student launch competition. Each year, the rocket must include a payload that accomplishes a mission in accordance with requirements set out in the handbook. In the 2021-2022 competition, the mission for the payload was to "design a payload capable of autonomously locating the launch vehicle upon landing by identifying the launch vehicleâ€™s grid position on an aerial image of the launch site without the use of a global positioning system (GPS)." Use of a compass or other earth-specific navigation methods were also prohibited, as the mission design was intended to emulate the challanges of navigating a rover on mars.

To complete this mission, myself and a team of four other members designed a system which used two 9-DoF inertial measurement units to collect three dimensional acceleration and rotation data (the magnatometer data was ignored to comply with the mission requirements). The data was saved to a file, and upon landing the data was integrated twice to calculate the flight path of the rocket. Once the flight path was known, the final landing coordinates were used to determine which grid square the rocket landed in, and the data was sent back to the ground station via a LoRa radio.
